🔬 Experiments

Step-by-step guides for all three fire experiments.

Proving that fire needs fuel, heat, and oxygen simultaneously
01
🧪 Materials Needed
  • Candle
  • Lighter or matches
  • Glass jars (different sizes)
  • CO₂ from baking soda + vinegar
  • Wet wood sample
  • Dry wood sample
  • Safety goggles
📋 Steps
  1. Set up your candle on a stable, fireproof surface and light it.
  2. Observe the flame burning freely — all three fire triangle elements are present.
  3. Cover the candle with a small glass jar and watch the flame shrink and go out as oxygen is consumed.
  4. Repeat with a larger jar to show it takes longer — more oxygen, more burn time.
  5. Prepare a CO₂ solution (baking soda + vinegar in a cup) and carefully pour the invisible gas over the flame to smother it.
  6. Compare trying to burn wet wood vs dry wood to demonstrate how fuel condition matters.
✅ Expected Result

The flame goes out every time one element is removed. This directly demonstrates the Fire Triangle theory — no oxygen = no fire, no matter how much fuel and heat are present.

⚠️ Keep a bowl of water nearby. Never leave a flame unattended. Perform on a fireproof surface.
🌡️ Flash Point vs. Ignition Point
Comparing how different substances respond to heat
02
🧪 Materials Needed
  • Rubbing alcohol (isopropanol)
  • Cooking oil (vegetable/canola)
  • Thermometer
  • Small metal tins / tin cans
  • Hot plate or stove
  • Lighter or long match
  • Safety goggles + gloves
📋 Steps
  1. Pour a small amount of rubbing alcohol in one tin and cooking oil in another. Label them clearly.
  2. Place the rubbing alcohol tin on the heat source and slowly increase the temperature while monitoring with a thermometer.
  3. At around 13°C (55°F), bring a flame near (not into) the surface — observe the brief flash of igniting vapors. This is the flash point.
  4. Note that the flame does not sustain — it requires a higher temperature (the fire point) to keep burning.
  5. Repeat with cooking oil — observe that vapors don't form until around 300°C (572°F), far higher than alcohol.
  6. Record both flash points and compare your results.
✅ Expected Result

Rubbing alcohol flashes at a much lower temperature than cooking oil, showing why some substances are far more fire-hazardous than others even at room temperature.

⚠️ Adult supervision required. Use a long lighter. Keep amounts small. Have a fire extinguisher or sand nearby.
🎨 Flame Color Chemistry
How metal salts produce different colored flames
03
🧪 Materials Needed
  • Wooden skewers / popsicle sticks
  • Water
  • Table salt (NaCl) → 🟡 Yellow
  • Copper sulfate → 🟢 Green
  • Potassium chloride → 🟣 Purple
  • Lithium chloride → 🔴 Red
  • Barium chloride → 🟢 Bright Green
  • Small cups (one per salt)
  • Bunsen burner or gas stove
  • Safety goggles + gloves
📋 Steps
  1. Dissolve each salt separately in a small cup of water to create individual salt solutions.
  2. Soak a wooden skewer in each solution for at least 5 minutes.
  3. Light your burner and hold the first soaked skewer in the flame. Observe the color.
  4. Let the flame return to normal between each test to avoid color mixing.
  5. Repeat for each salt solution, recording the color produced each time.
  6. Compare your results with the expected colors and note any differences.
✅ Expected Result

Each metal salt produces a distinct flame color because different metal ions release different amounts of energy as light when heated. This is the same principle used in fireworks manufacturing.

⚠️ Some metal salts can be toxic. Wear gloves, don't inhale fumes, and wash hands thoroughly after handling.
